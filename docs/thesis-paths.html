1. How to read this document

A few up-front honesties before the substance.

I am a language model, not a PhD committee. I have read a lot of ML papers and I know the AI-weather-modeling literature reasonably well. I know less about the lived experience of doing a climate-science or applied-ML PhD in the US system in 2026, and I know less still about the political dynamics of your specific advisor, department, and committee. Where I'm guessing on those, I'll say so.

I have biases. I tend to weigh methodological novelty higher than evaluation contributions, because that is what the ML literature rewards. Atmospheric science weights things differently — long empirical evaluation chapters can be the heart of a thesis there in a way they wouldn't be in a CS thesis. Calibrate against your advisor's actual preferences, not against my biases.

None of these four options is wrong. Each one is a defensible thesis. The question isn't which is best in the abstract; the question is which fits the kind of researcher you want to become.

You don't have to pick now. The first 6–12 months of a PhD are explicitly for figuring out which question grabs you, not for locking in a plan. The "what to do in the next six months" section at the end is the actually-actionable part. Everything before it is scenario planning.

2. The five dimensions that should drive the choice

Before getting into specific options, here are the dimensions along which they really differ. If you read this document and the choice still feels muddy, the resolution is probably to figure out where you sit on each of these five.

2.1 Advisor's expertise

This is the strongest constraint and the one most students underweight. Your advisor will sign your thesis, attend your defense, write your recommendation letters, and route you to a postdoc or industry job. If your thesis sits in a region they are not comfortable with, you get worse mentorship, worse letters, and worse positioning.

Rough mapping:

If your advisor is…The natural fits are…
A climate scientist who works on monsoonsOption A or Option C
A climate scientist who works on model evaluation more broadlyOption A, with potential to pivot to D
An ML researcher focused on weather/climate as an applicationOption B or D
An applied-math / computational scientistOption D, possibly B
An operationally-minded person (NWP center, climate services)Option B, possibly C
Cross-listed: ML and climateAny; you have the most freedom

If you're already committed to Hunt as advisor, his prior framing matters more than this table. Hunt suggested three precondition tests; that's implicitly closer to Option A or C in flavor. But he likely doesn't care which exact path you end up on as long as the work is good.

2.2 What kind of contribution feels satisfying to you

There are roughly four kinds of contribution a thesis can make. They sound similar but the day-to-day work is very different.

  • Evaluation contribution — "Here is how a class of methods/models actually behaves on important problems X, Y, Z." Day to day: a lot of running models, a lot of careful comparison, a lot of plot-making. Satisfying if you like getting the empirical answer right.
  • Methodological contribution — "Here is a new way to do X that has better properties than existing ways." Day to day: architecting and training models, designing experiments to isolate what's working. Satisfying if you like building things that work better.
  • Theoretical contribution — "Here is why the current way doesn't work, in principle." Day to day: a lot of reading math, a lot of careful arguments, smaller-scale empirical work designed to test specific claims. Satisfying if you like understanding things at a deeper level than "it works."
  • Synthetic / decision-framework contribution — "Here is how to decide between methods X, Y, Z for purposes A, B, C." Day to day: lots of breadth, comparison, framework-design. Less common in CS / atmospheric science PhDs; more common in policy, operations research, decision sciences.

The four options below map roughly:

  • Option A → evaluation contribution.
  • Option B → methodological contribution.
  • Option C → synthetic / decision-framework contribution.
  • Option D → theoretical contribution (with empirical support).

The biggest predictor of whether a PhD goes well is whether the student is doing the kind of contribution they personally find satisfying. The opposite — a student who wants to build things but is doing an evaluation thesis, or who wants to evaluate but is doing theoretical work — produces years of grinding misery and often delayed defenses.

2.3 Risk tolerance

Different options have different expected-value vs variance profiles.

OptionExpected outcomeVariance
ASolid evaluation thesis; multiple workshop / journal papersLow — you can almost always grind out the evaluation
BMethodological contribution if Ch 3 hits; competent applied work if notMedium — Ch 3 is where the variance lives
CSynthesis paper plus three prototypesMedium — could feel "thin" in a worst case
DCould be a celebrated theoretical contribution; could be three years of grinding on a question that doesn't yield a clean resultHigh

Risk tolerance is partly personal and partly about your safety net. First-generation graduate students, students supporting families, students on visa timelines that don't tolerate an extra year — these all shift you toward lower variance. Students with a clear academic-career appetite and patient advisors / families can take more variance because the upside on D is potentially higher.

2.4 Time horizon

US-style atmospheric-science PhDs run 4–6 years. ML-focused PhDs run 4–5 years. Where you can shave time off without losing thesis quality varies by option:

  • Option A is the most clock-predictable. The work scales linearly with effort.
  • Option B's MVP chapters are predictable; the methodologically-novel Ch 3 can take 1–2 years of trying things that don't work before something does.
  • Option C is fast if executed cleanly (~3 years possible) but more typically expands as each prototype reveals follow-up questions.
  • Option D is the least predictable. A theoretical thesis can defend in 4 years or take 7. The variance is in whether the right conceptual breakthrough actually comes.

2.5 Job market positioning

What you want to do after the PhD shapes which thesis is best to do during it. Rough guide for 2026–2030 weather/climate ML landscape:

  • Industry AI weather/climate (Google, NVIDIA, ECMWF research, Microsoft, climate-tech startups): Options A and B both work. B is slightly stronger if the methodological contribution is novel; A is stronger if you want to position for evaluation-team roles.
  • Academic faculty in atmospheric science: Option A is the most traditional. C is harder to place but possible. B and D are excellent if you want a ML-focused department (rarer in atmospheric science but growing).
  • Academic faculty in ML applied to climate: Options B and D. A pure evaluation thesis is a tougher hire in ML departments, even ones friendly to applications.
  • National lab or NWP center (NCAR, NOAA, ECMWF, JMA, IMD, UK Met Office): Any option, but A and C resonate particularly well because evaluation and decision-support work is central to those organizations' missions.
  • Climate-policy or climate-services adjacent careers: Option C is the most directly relevant; A as a secondary fit.
  • Quant / finance / ML-adjacent industry not specifically in weather/climate: Honestly, any option works because they care about whether you can think rigorously, not the topic. B and D positioning best for these because they signal methodological/theoretical depth.

3. Option A — Evaluation framework for AI weather over tropical convection

The most direct extension of Proto 1. The thesis question reframes "does AIFS bias have learnable structure" into "across the class of AI weather models, what is the bias structure over the world's tropical convective regions, and what does it imply for operational use?"

3.1 The thesis question, sharpened

"Global AI weather models systematically misrepresent convective precipitation in the tropics, with structured biases that vary by region and by storm regime, and these biases meaningfully limit operational use unless explicitly corrected."

This is a thesis-shaped claim. It is specific enough to be falsifiable ("structured biases that vary by region" — show or fail to show that), ambitious enough to matter (tropical convection is where global models struggle most, and the operational stakes — agriculture, water, disaster preparedness — are highest), and bounded enough to be doable in 3–4 years.

3.2 Chapter-by-chapter breakdown

Chapter 1 — A reusable bias evaluation framework

Build the infrastructure (much of which already exists from Proto 1) for a uniform comparison of multiple AI weather models across multiple years and multiple regions. Concretely:

  • Models: AIFS, AIFS-ENS, GraphCast, Pangu, FourCastNet-Spherical, and at least one operational baseline (ECMWF HRES). Five to six models in total.
  • Regions: Three or four tropical regions with distinct convective regimes — Indian monsoon (Proto 1 region; you already have the pipeline), West African monsoon (different orographic geometry, different moisture source, different aerosol environment), Maritime Continent / Indonesia (the hardest case for any model — small islands, complex orography, diurnal cycle driven), and optionally Amazon basin (different from monsoonal but representative of tropical convection more broadly).
  • Years: 5–10 years for each region, depending on data availability. IMERG goes back to 2000; ERA5 to 1940; high- quality gauge networks vary.
  • Diagnostics: Generalized from Proto 1 — mean bias maps, RMSE maps, regional stratifications, regime stratifications (BSISO for the Indian monsoon, West African Monsoon Index for West Africa, MJO RMM as a regime variable that crosses regions), elevation-stratified bias, rainfall-magnitude stratified bias.

This chapter is mostly engineering effort on top of Proto 1. Contribution: a community-usable evaluation framework, plus the first published cross-model cross-region tropical convection evaluation at this scale.

Chapter 2 — What drives the bias structure

This is the hard chapter and the one that elevates the thesis from "evaluation" to "evaluation with insight." You cannot retrain the big models (you don't have their training infrastructure or budget), so "ablation" here means analytical decomposition rather than direct ablation:

  • Loss function decomposition. AIFS, GraphCast, etc. train predominantly with MSE-like losses on autoregressive rollouts. There is good theoretical reason to expect this to drive conditional-mean prediction (i.e., narrow distributions). You can demonstrate this empirically by training a smaller controlled model (e.g., StormCast at regional scale, or a custom small UNet) with MSE vs quantile vs CRPS losses on the same data and showing distribution-shape changes.
  • Training-data smoothing decomposition. ERA5 is itself biased over India and the tropics (Proto 1 demonstrated this). To what extent are AIFS's biases inherited from ERA5 versus introduced by the model? Compute the AIFS−ERA5 residual across your full evaluation set and characterize its structure.
  • Architectural comparison. AIFS uses a graph architecture, GraphCast a different graph architecture, Pangu transformers, FourCastNet-Spherical Fourier neural operators. Where their biases agree, blame is on training data or training objective. Where they diverge, architecture matters. The pattern of agreement is itself the result of this chapter.
  • Resolution decomposition. Compare against a high- resolution downscaled or convection-permitting model (e.g., GraphCast-Operational at 0.25° vs higher-resolution regional models where available). Bias structure that disappears with resolution you attribute to coarse-grid orography smoothing; bias structure that persists you attribute to deeper model issues.

The honest difficulty here is that none of these decompositions is clean. You will get correlational evidence, not causal evidence. That's okay — make the limits of the claims explicit, and design the analysis to give the most informative correlational evidence possible. The contribution is the first systematic attempt at this kind of decomposition.

Chapter 3 — Corrective head as a downstream remedy

Essentially the corrective head MVP from the immediate follow-on, expanded to multiple models and multiple regions. Concretely:

  • Tabular MVP (gradient-boosted trees, LightGBM with quantile loss at first; then a small CNN with spatial context once the MVP is settled).
  • Trained on India 2025 → tested on India 2024 (year transfer).
  • Trained on India → tested on West Africa (region transfer).
  • Trained on AIFS → applied to GraphCast (model transfer; tests whether the correction captures something AIFS-specific or weather-model-class-general).

Comparison: corrected forecast vs raw AIFS on the same diagnostics from Chapter 1. Headline metric: heavy-rain detection rate (already discussed), plus CRPS improvement and calibration of the corrected probabilistic output.

Chapter 4 — Operational implications and a deployable artifact

The final chapter takes the corrected forecasts and asks the real question: do they improve any downstream user's decision? Concrete candidates:

  • Reservoir management: Take the corrected forecasts, feed them into a simplified reservoir-operations model, show that decisions based on the corrected forecast lead to better outcomes (less spillage, fewer outage events) than decisions based on the raw forecast.
  • Agricultural decisions: Same idea, simpler user model — would a farmer who plants vs delays based on the forecast end up better off?
  • Flood warning: Skill in the heavy-rain detection metric translates into hit rate and false-alarm rate for flood warnings.

A small amount of decision-relevant analysis here goes a long way, because it gives the thesis a clear operational anchor. This is the chapter that distinguishes "publishable evaluation" from "evaluation the community actually cares about."

3.3 Where the contributions live

  • Chapter 1: Empirical contribution — the bias characterization itself plus a reusable framework.
  • Chapter 2: Analytical contribution — the decomposition methodology.
  • Chapter 3: Methodological contribution — the corrective head and its transfer properties.
  • Chapter 4: Applied contribution — the downstream-decision evaluation.

Four chapters, four kinds of contribution. This is what makes it robust as a thesis — even if one chapter is weaker, the other three carry the work.

3.5 Where this competes with existing work

The risk for Option A is that "AI weather model evaluation" is a crowded space in 2026. ECMWF, NVIDIA, Microsoft, NCAR, and individual academic groups all publish evaluation papers regularly. To not get scooped, you need a defensible angle. The angles I'd consider:

  • Tropical convection specifically. The published evaluation literature is overwhelmingly Northern Hemisphere extratropical. A thesis-scale effort focused on tropical convection fills a real gap.
  • Cross-region comparison. Most published work is single-region. A four-region cross-comparison is novel.
  • The decomposition methodology in Chapter 2. If you can pull off a clean analytical decomposition of bias drivers, that's a methodological contribution worth a strong paper.
  • The decision-relevant evaluation in Chapter 4. Almost nobody in ML weather modeling does this. It's a sharp differentiator.

3.6 Failure modes

  • You spend years generating careful evaluations that are then obviated by a new model release. Mitigation: design Chapter 1's framework so adding a new model is a few days, not months. Adding a new model release should be a robustness check, not a setback.
  • Chapter 2's decomposition turns out to be too noisy to support clean conclusions. Mitigation: scope Chapter 2 narrower if needed (focus on loss function decomposition only, with the small-model ablation being the main evidence). The thesis still works without a tight Chapter 2.
  • Chapter 4 ends up being a small pilot rather than a substantive analysis. Mitigation: pick one of the three application candidates upfront and partner with someone in that domain. A single deep application is better than three shallow ones.
  • The thesis reads as four loosely connected papers rather than a coherent program. Mitigation: write Chapter 1's framework explicitly so that Chapters 2–4 build on it. The framework is the spine.

3.7 Who this fits

The student who would thrive in Option A:

  • Likes systems work and infrastructure, doesn't mind grinding through evaluation runs.
  • Has a meteorology or climate science background (or the motivation to acquire one).
  • Wants a strong applied or national-lab career, or a faculty job in an atmospheric-science department.
  • Is risk-averse and wants high confidence of finishing in 4 years.
  • Has an advisor who is themselves an evaluation-oriented climate scientist.

4. Option B — Corrective heads as a methodological contribution

The product-shaped path. The thesis reframes as "global AI weather models produce useful but biased forecasts; a class of small downstream models can correct the biases in principled ways, with theory and practice that generalize across models and regions."

4.1 The thesis question, sharpened

"Corrective models trained on global-AI-weather-model output produce substantial skill gains over the raw forecasts, with architectural and training-objective choices that generalize beyond a single region and a single source model."

The implicit ambition of this thesis is methodological — by the end of it, the field should have a better understanding of how to do downstream correction of AI weather model output. Not just "we corrected one model in one place" but "here are the principles."

4.2 Chapter-by-chapter breakdown

Chapter 1 — Motivation and baseline characterization

Proto 1 condensed, plus extension to one or two additional models (GraphCast minimum) to motivate that the bias pattern generalizes. This is the "the problem exists and is worth fixing" chapter.

Chapter 2 — A principled MVP corrective head

The careful baseline. Tabular gradient-boosted regression with the features and quantile loss discussed earlier. The careful part is in the experiments:

  • Feature ablations. Which features actually contribute, in what proportion? Validation that the model is exploiting the structural patterns from Chapter 1.
  • Loss function ablations. MSE vs Huber vs quantile vs weighted vs CRPS. The differences matter most in the tail bins.
  • Spatial holdout vs random holdout vs temporal holdout. Different holdouts answer different questions about generalization. Doing all three gives a much richer picture of where the model transfers and where it doesn't.
  • Comparison to traditional MOS (Model Output Statistics). Traditional NWP post-processing has decades of methodology; cite it, compare against it, position your corrective head as the AI-era successor.

This chapter alone is a strong workshop or methods-track paper. The thesis-shaped version is to make it the careful baseline for everything that follows.

Chapter 3 — A genuinely novel architecture

This is the high-variance chapter and the one that determines whether the thesis has a real methodological contribution or is "applied LightGBM." Candidates I'd consider, ordered by ambition:

3.a Spatial-context UNet with quantile head. Takes a window of the AIFS field plus static features, outputs a corrected field with calibrated quantiles. This is the obvious next step beyond the MVP. The contribution is in making it work for tropical convective extremes, which standard UNet training does not naturally do.

3.b Conditional score-based / diffusion correction. Train a diffusion model conditional on AIFS output and static features to sample from the conditional distribution of true rainfall. This is the strongest match to the "narrow predictive distribution" problem Proto 1 surfaced — diffusion models can produce calibrated extreme draws. The contribution is genuine novelty: nobody has shipped a diffusion-based correction model for AI weather output (as of my knowledge). It's also the most likely to fail.

3.c Equivariant or physics-informed architecture. Build in inductive biases — rotational equivariance to make corrections consistent under coordinate changes, or physics constraints (mass conservation, positive precipitation, spatial smoothness penalties). Contribution: showing that domain-specific inductive bias improves generalization to new regions.

3.d Multi-task probabilistic correction. Train a correction model that outputs not just point predictions but parameters of a distribution (or a quantile fan). Contribution: the calibration analysis matters, the distributional skill matters, the connection to operational probabilistic forecasting is direct.

The smart move is to pick one of these (probably 3.b — diffusion — if you have the appetite, or 3.d if you want lower risk) and execute it deeply. Trying to ship all four is a sub-thesis-per-chapter trap.

Chapter 4 — Cross-model, cross-region transfer

Apply the architecture from Chapter 3 across (model, region) pairs:

  • Train on AIFS-India, evaluate on AIFS-West-Africa (region transfer).
  • Train on AIFS-India, evaluate on GraphCast-India (model transfer).
  • Train on AIFS-India, evaluate on GraphCast-West-Africa (both).
  • Train jointly on multiple (model, region) pairs, evaluate on a held-out pair (multi-task transfer).

The contribution is characterizing how well corrections transfer across these dimensions. The published transfer-learning literature for weather model correction is sparse; clean results here are highly citable.

Chapter 5 — Operational deployment and what it costs

The deployment chapter. Inference cost (how fast does correction run on top of AIFS), latency, integration with operational pipelines. Real or simulated operational deployment with a partner organization if possible (NCAR, ECMWF, IMD, or a startup like Atmo or Tomorrow.io).

This chapter is what gets you noticed by the operational community. A thesis without it is "interesting research"; with it, it's "someone who could ship this in production."

  • Chapter 3: Methodological contribution (the novel architecture). This is the one that determines the thesis's lasting impact.
  • Chapter 4: Empirical contribution — the transfer characterization.
  • Chapter 5: Applied / engineering contribution.

4.5 Where this competes with existing work

Option B competes with:

  • Industry post-processing teams. Google, NVIDIA, ECMWF, and several startups are working on similar corrections. They have more compute and more engineering resources than you. What you have that they don't: time to do something carefully, freedom to publish, and the ability to focus on the methodologically interesting parts rather than the operational/commercial parts.
  • Academic post-processing groups in NWP. Long tradition; Stephane Vannitsem, Sebastian Lerch, others. They typically work on statistical post-processing of physics-based models. Your angle is post-processing of AI weather models, which is a fresh subfield. Most of their methods are likely transferable.
  • Traditional downscaling / bias correction in climate science. Decades of work. Most of it is regression-based and operates on monthly/seasonal scales rather than the per-day, per-cell granularity you'd be working at. Some overlap; should be cited; not directly competitive.

The defensible angle is the combination of (a) AI weather model output as the target of correction, (b) the architectural novelty in Chapter 3, and (c) the transfer characterization in Chapter 4. Without those, you risk being seen as "doing post-processing in 2026."

4.6 Failure modes

  • Chapter 3's novel architecture doesn't beat the MVP from Chapter 2. This is the dominant risk. Mitigation: structure Chapter 3 so that even if the headline architecture doesn't win, you still have a clean methodological contribution about when it does and doesn't work, and what that implies for downstream model design. "We tried this and here is why it didn't work" is a thesis-worthy result if the analysis of why is rigorous.
  • Industry ships a better corrective system before you can publish. Mitigation: focus on aspects that industry typically doesn't publish — careful ablations, transfer characterization, theory. Your contribution is the systematic understanding, not the deployed product.
  • Cross-region transfer doesn't work. This is actually a positive result if you frame it right ("here is why region-specific correction is necessary, with characterization") but it can feel like a failure if you went into Chapter 4 expecting positive transfer.
  • Chapter 5 partnerships don't materialize. National lab or industry partnerships take 6–12 months of negotiation. Start cultivating them in Year 2.

4.7 Who this fits

  • The student who likes building things that work better.
  • Comfortable with ML systems work and not afraid of training new models from scratch.
  • Wants industry post-PhD with maybe a faculty fallback, or faculty in an ML/CS department with a climate focus.
  • Medium-to-high risk tolerance (Chapter 3 is the variance chapter).
  • Has an advisor who supports ambitious methodological work and has at least passing familiarity with diffusion or generative methods (or is comfortable letting you collaborate with someone who does).

5. Option C — Diagnostic studies as a decision framework

The Hunt-shaped thesis. The thesis question is meta: "given that there are multiple defensible operational research directions for applying AI to monsoon forecasting (corrective heads, regime-conditioned ensembles, climate emulator climatology), which should the community invest in, and what does the diagnostic evidence say?"

5.1 The thesis question, sharpened

"The choice between three plausible operational futures for AI weather modeling in the Indian monsoon (corrective post-processing of deterministic forecasts; regime-conditioned ensemble weighting; AI climate emulator climatology) is best made empirically on the basis of three independent precondition tests, which this thesis conducts and synthesizes."

This is unusual as a thesis question. Most theses make a single substantive claim; this one makes a framework claim. The risk is that a committee responds "this is a survey, not original research." The defense against that risk is the rigor of the three precondition tests and the sharpness of the synthesis.

5.2 Chapter-by-chapter breakdown

Chapter 1 — Framing and the three operational futures

The literature review and framing chapter. Why these three futures and not others. What's at stake operationally. What a precondition test means and why each operational future has one. This is the chapter that has to convince a committee that the rest of the thesis isn't just three loosely connected projects.

Chapter 2 — Proto 1 expanded (corrective head precondition)

The Proto 1 work, finished and expanded. Three findings, characterized across two years (2024 and 2025) and at least two models (AIFS and either GraphCast or AIFS-ENS). The precondition test: does the bias have learnable structure?

Result framing: yes / no / partial, with quantification. If yes, corrective post-processing is justified.

Chapter 3 — Proto 2 (regime-conditioned ensemble precondition)

Run two probabilistic models (AIFSENS and either GenCast or a constructed ensemble of perturbed deterministic AIFS runs). Compute CRPS at each lead. Stratify by atmospheric regime — start with MJO RMM since BSISO is blocked; add self-computed BSISO if it cleanly works. Compare model ranking across regimes.

Result framing: do the model rankings change with regime? If yes, regime-conditioned ensemble weighting is justified.

Chapter 4 — Proto 3 (climate emulator climatology precondition)

Run ACE2 (or DLESyM) for 10 years, ideally with 3–5 seeds. Compare monsoon climatology (mean rainfall, seasonal cycle, onset date, trough position) against GPCC and ERA5. The precondition test: does the emulator reproduce monsoon climatology approximately?

Result framing: yes / no / interesting failure. Any of the three is a useful finding.

Chapter 5 — Synthesis and decision framework

The integrative chapter. Take the three precondition results and construct a decision framework: under what circumstances should the community invest in each operational future. This is the chapter that determines whether the thesis adds up to more than its parts.

Concretely:

  • Cost-benefit framework: development time, compute requirements, operational integration cost, expected skill improvement.
  • Regime-of-validity for each operational future based on the precondition results.
  • Sensitivity to assumptions: under what conditions would the decision change?
  • Implications for stakeholders: NWP centers, climate services, end users.

Chapter 6 — A representative downstream demonstration

Optional but valuable. Pick the operational future the decision framework most strongly endorses and build a representative version of it. This grounds the thesis in tangible artifact-form output.

5.3 Where the contributions live

  • Chapters 2–4: Empirical contributions — three self-contained precondition results.
  • Chapter 5: Synthetic contribution — the decision framework.
  • Chapter 6 (if included): Applied contribution.

The synthesis chapter is where the thesis lives or dies. A weak synthesis makes the thesis feel like three separate projects in a folder. A strong synthesis is genuinely novel — there is no published "how to choose between AI operational futures for monsoon forecasting" framework.

5.5 Where this competes with existing work

Lower competition than A or B — nobody is doing exactly this. The risk isn't being scooped; the risk is being seen as "not real research" by a traditional committee.

Closest existing work: review articles and perspective pieces in journals like Bulletin of the AMS, Reviews of Geophysics, Nature Reviews Earth & Environment. Your thesis would be a much more rigorous version of that genre — actual experiments rather than synthesis of others' results, with a clear decision-framework output.

5.6 Failure modes

  • The synthesis chapter doesn't add up. This is the dominant risk. Mitigation: design the synthesis from the start. Decide in Year 1 what the decision framework will look like at a high level and let that shape how you design each of the three precondition tests.
  • One of the precondition tests yields an ambiguous result. Mitigation: design each precondition test to produce a useful finding regardless of which way it goes (Proto 3's "interesting failure mode" framing is the right instinct).
  • Committee reads it as a survey. Mitigation: lead with the decision framework throughout the writing. The three preconditions are evidence; the framework is the contribution.
  • The community moves on from one of the three preconditions before you finish. Mitigation: pick preconditions for operational futures that the community is genuinely uncertain about, not ones that will be obviously settled by the time you defend.

5.7 Who this fits

  • Likes breadth more than depth.
  • Wants to defend reasonably quickly and move on.
  • Career goal is operational, policy, climate services, or national lab rather than research faculty.
  • Has an advisor who understands the value of synthesis and is willing to defend the thesis shape to traditional committee members.
  • Low-to-medium risk tolerance.

6. Option D — A theoretical pivot on why predictive distributions narrow

The most ambitious and the highest-variance option. Reframes Proto 1's finding as a question of fundamental ML methodology: why do autoregressive deep-learning weather models produce narrow predictive distributions, and what can be done about it at the architecture / training / theory level?

6.1 The thesis question, sharpened

"Autoregressive deep-learning weather models trained with MSE-class losses systematically converge to conditional-mean prediction. This distributional collapse has identifiable causes — in the loss function, the training data distribution, and the model architecture — and can be mitigated through training-objective design, generative modeling, and explicit distributional modeling."

This is the most thesis-shaped of the four options, in the sense that it makes a substantive, falsifiable, ML-methodologically interesting claim. It is also the most likely to take 6 years and the most likely to depend on a single key insight that you may or may not have during your PhD.

6.2 Chapter-by-chapter breakdown

Chapter 1 — Empirical motivation

Proto 1, extended to other models and to controlled small-scale experiments. Demonstrate the narrow-distribution phenomenon robustly across architectures and training regimes. The contribution is the systematic empirical characterization — showing this isn't an AIFS quirk but a class-wide phenomenon.

Chapter 2 — Why this happens, in principle

The theoretical / analytical chapter. Possible directions:

  • MSE loss drives conditional-mean prediction — a statement that is true in single-step regression but harder to pin down formally in autoregressive rollout settings. The contribution is making this rigorous: prove or empirically demonstrate that for autoregressive forecasting models, MSE training on rollouts of length N produces strictly narrower distributions than alternative training schemes.
  • Iterative noise amplification under autoregression. Each step's stochasticity gets averaged out in the conditional mean prediction, so multi-step rollouts produce distributions that are much narrower than warranted by the true stochasticity of the system.
  • Training data smoothing. ERA5 is an analysis product that already smooths sub-grid stochasticity. Models trained on it inherit the smoothing.
  • Architectural constraints. Most current AI weather models lack any stochastic latent variables; they are deterministic functions of the input. This constrains the distribution shape to whatever the deterministic function class can produce.

The strongest version of this chapter argues for all four causes in combination, with empirical or theoretical evidence for each. The weakest version picks one and goes deep.

Chapter 3 — Training-objective contributions

Design and validate training objectives that produce calibrated distributions. Candidates:

  • CRPS as the training loss. CRPS is naturally distributional. Train AI weather models with CRPS instead of MSE and characterize the resulting distribution shapes.
  • Quantile regression. Output multiple quantiles rather than a single point prediction. Connection to operational probabilistic forecasting is direct.
  • Distributional matching losses. Train the model so that the distribution of its output matches the distribution of the target, not just point-by-point.
  • Adversarial losses. A discriminator that distinguishes model output from real observations. Encourages realism in the distributional sense.

The contribution is a systematic understanding of which training objectives produce which distribution properties, with concrete recommendations for the field.

Chapter 4 — Architectural contributions

Design and validate architectures that produce calibrated distributions. Candidates:

  • Conditional score-based / diffusion weather models. The state-of-the-art generative approach. GenCast is an example that already exists; the contribution would be a deeper architectural / theoretical understanding of why and when it works.
  • VAE-style stochastic weather models. Latent variable models that explicitly sample stochasticity at each step.
  • Multi-head probabilistic prediction. The architecture outputs parameters of a distribution at each grid cell at each timestep, learned end-to-end.
  • Stochastic perturbation methods. Following operational NWP practice (Stochastic Kinetic Energy Backscatter, etc.), introduce stochasticity at each step in a principled way.

Chapter 5 — Empirical validation on the Indian monsoon

Apply the new training objectives and architectures to Proto 1's setup. Show that the calibration problems Proto 1 identified are mitigated. This grounds the theoretical work in the original empirical motivation.

Chapter 6 — Implications for AI weather modeling more broadly

Synthesis chapter. Position the contributions in the broader landscape of AI weather modeling. What is now known, what remains open, what recommendations follow for the field.

6.3 Where the contributions live

  • Chapter 1: Empirical contribution — the phenomenon characterization.
  • Chapter 2: Theoretical / analytical contribution — the diagnosis.
  • Chapter 3: Methodological contribution — the training objectives.
  • Chapter 4: Methodological contribution — the architectures.
  • Chapter 5: Empirical contribution — the validation.
  • Chapter 6: Synthesis.

This is the most ambitious option in terms of number and type of contributions. It is also the most likely to overrun.

6.4 Realistic compute and time costs

The honest answer is that compute and time are highly variable for Option D. If Chapter 2 yields a clean theoretical result early, the rest is fast. If it doesn't, you could spend years on Chapters 3 and 4 trying to find a clean methodological contribution.

6.5 Where this competes with existing work

The diffusion-model-for-weather angle is very active. GenCast (Google DeepMind), Aurora (Microsoft), and several academic groups are working on generative weather models. Your contribution can't be "we built a diffusion weather model" because they already exist. The contribution has to be deeper:

  • A theoretical understanding of why diffusion / generative approaches work for this problem and when they don't.
  • A systematic comparison of training objectives that previous work hasn't done.
  • A novel architecture or training recipe that improves on the state of the art.

Honestly, this is the option most likely to feel like you're chasing industry. Mitigation: focus on the analytical / theoretical parts of Chapter 2, which industry doesn't typically publish, and on the careful empirical comparisons in Chapter 5, which industry rarely does in a thesis-worthy way.

6.6 Failure modes

  • Chapter 2's theory doesn't come together. Mitigation: have a fallback that makes Chapter 2 a careful empirical investigation rather than a theoretical proof.
  • Chapter 4's architecture doesn't beat baselines. Same mitigation as Option B Chapter 3 — make the failure analysis itself the contribution.
  • Industry ships a strictly better version before you can publish. This is the dominant risk. Mitigation: focus on aspects industry doesn't publish (theory, careful comparisons).
  • The thesis takes 7 years. Honestly this is hard to mitigate structurally; the mitigation is psychological — go into Option D knowing this could be a 6–7 year program.

6.7 Who this fits

  • The student with a strong ML theory background (or strong appetite to develop one).
  • Wants to do ambitious methodological work that could be paradigm-shifting.
  • Wants academic faculty in an ML or applied-ML department.
  • Has very high risk tolerance and a flexible timeline.
  • Has an advisor who can mentor on ML methodology and theory (not just applications) and is comfortable with high-variance research.

7. Hybrid combinations actually worth considering

The four options are not exclusive. Several combinations make more sense as a thesis than any single option.

7.1 A + B — Evaluation framework grounding a methodological contribution

Take Option A's chapters 1–2 (evaluation framework + decomposition) as the empirical foundation, then Option B's chapters 3–4 (novel architecture + transfer) as the methodological contribution.

This is probably the most defensible single thesis shape. The evaluation chapters set up the corrective head chapters as solving a documented and important problem. The methodological chapters give the thesis its central contribution. Total 4 chapters, doable in 4 years.

7.2 B + D — Theoretical underpinnings for methodological work

Option D's Chapter 2 (theoretical diagnosis) precedes Option B's Chapter 3 (novel architecture). The theory motivates the architectural choice; the architecture validates the theory.

This is the strongest combination for an ML methodologist. The theoretical insight is principled and the architecture is the applied test of the theory. Risk: still 5+ years.

7.3 A + D — Evaluation that motivates a theoretical question

Option A's Chapter 1 (evaluation framework) reveals the narrow-distribution problem cross-model, cross-region. Option D's Chapters 2–4 (theory + objectives + architectures) address it. Chapter 5 returns to the evaluation framework to validate.

Similar to B+D but with a stronger empirical grounding. Good fit if your advisor wants substantial empirical work but you want methodological depth.

7.4 C as a sequence of papers, A or B as the thesis

A pragmatic option: do Proto 1, Proto 2, Proto 3 as standalone publications during the early PhD years. Use them to figure out which operational future has the most research-grade potential. Pivot to that one (A, B, or D) for the main thesis body.

This is what many students actually do whether or not they plan to. The PhD ends up being a sequence of explorations followed by a focused contribution. The risk is that the explorations consume too much time and you defend late or with a thinner main contribution.

8. Failure modes and red flags

8.1 Patterns that lead to slow PhDs across all options

  • Endless infrastructure work. If you're three years in and still mostly building pipelines, you have a problem. The pipeline work in Proto 1 is appropriate for year 1; not for year 3.
  • Scope creep on Chapter N because Chapter N-1 wasn't tight enough. Each chapter should have a defined endpoint. If you keep going back to add to Chapter 2 while writing Chapter 3, the thesis won't finish.
  • The "I'll write the paper after I finish the analysis" trap. Analysis without writing expands forever. Write early drafts in parallel with analysis.
  • Avoiding the hardest chapter. Every option has one chapter that is the hard one. Students often drift away from it. The hard chapter is usually the one that makes the thesis thesis-shaped.
  • Following the model release cycle. If you keep rerunning everything because there's a new AIFS or GraphCast release, you never finish. Pick a snapshot, document it, and move on. New releases become future-work or robustness checks, not setbacks.

8.2 Red flags specific to each option

Option A: Three years in with no clear methodological contribution. You can grind out evaluations forever; at some point you need something the field will cite for the methodology, not just the data. If by mid-year-3 you don't have an analytical or methodological hook, push toward one.

Option B: Chapter 3 keeps not working. If at month 18 of working on the novel architecture nothing has beaten the MVP, seriously consider pivoting Chapter 3's framing to "here is why this is hard, here is what doesn't work, here are the conditions under which it might." That can be a thesis chapter; "I'm still trying" can't.

Option C: The synthesis chapter doesn't have a clear framework. If by year 3 the three preconditions look like three independent papers rather than parts of a decision framework, get the framework on paper now. If you can't, the thesis is in trouble.

Option D: Year 4 and no clean theoretical result. The theoretical chapter is the highest-variance one. If by year 4 it's still in motion, you have to either reframe it as empirical or extend the PhD. There is no rescue plan that's quick.

8.3 Red flags about your advisor

These apply regardless of which option you pick:

  • Advisor consistently uninterested in the technical details of your work.
  • Advisor doesn't push back on weak parts of your drafts.
  • Advisor's other students consistently take 7+ years to defend.
  • Advisor has stopped publishing in your area.
  • Advisor pushes you toward fashionable topics that don't match your interests.

If two or more of these apply, the thesis-shape question is less important than the advisor question. Solve the advisor question first.

9. Career implications and post-PhD landing

9.1 The 2026–2030 landscape, briefly

Useful context: the field of "AI applied to weather and climate" is rapidly maturing and bifurcating. Three rough tracks:

  • Industry AI weather/climate. Google (DeepMind weather), NVIDIA (Earth-2), Microsoft (Aurora), ECMWF (operational AI), and a layer of climate-tech startups (Atmospheric Discovery, Tomorrow.io, Salient, Atmo, etc.). Growing. Pays well. Methodological and engineering work both valued.
  • National labs / NWP centers. NCAR, NOAA, JPL, ECMWF research, JMA, IMD, UK Met Office. Stable. Long-running research programs. Evaluation and decision-support work both valued.
  • Academic faculty. Atmospheric science departments increasingly hire ML-oriented faculty. ML/CS departments increasingly accept climate-applied work in their hiring searches. Both routes are open but the specific positioning matters.

10. What to do in the next six months regardless

The most useful concrete advice in this document: there is a sequence of moves that helps regardless of which option you eventually pick.

10.1 Months 1–2: finish Proto 1 properly

  • Finish the 122-day AIFS batch. ~$15 more GPU.
  • Re-run the analysis and finalize all seven figures.
  • Add bootstrap confidence intervals to the bin-level findings.
  • Add a land mask to the maps.
  • Polish WRITEUP.md into a draft workshop paper. Target: Climate Change AI workshop at NeurIPS 2026 or the AI for Weather workshop, whichever has the right deadline.

End state: a defendable, publishable artifact that's done.

10.2 Months 2–4: build the corrective head MVP

  • Implement the LightGBM tabular MVP from the immediate follow-on plan.
  • Train on India 2025, evaluate on a held-out region.
  • Compare against raw AIFS using the Proto 1 diagnostics.
  • Write up as a second workshop paper or as the start of a methods-track journal paper.

This is roughly the Option B Chapter 2 work. It also serves as the sanity check for whether Option B's full thesis is right for you: if you find this work satisfying and the headline metric improves meaningfully, B becomes much more plausible.

10.3 Months 3–4 (in parallel): the smoke tests

  • Run the ACE2 smoke test (30 minutes + a 4-hour rollout). Look at its monsoon climatology. This tells you whether Option C's Chapter 4 (climate emulator) is even possible.
  • Run a perturbed-IC ensemble of 5 deterministic AIFS runs and compute CRPS for a few dates. This tells you whether Option C's Chapter 3 (regime-conditioned ensemble) is tractable on your budget.
  • Either of these is two days of work. Doing both lets you make informed decisions about Option C without committing to it.

10.4 Months 4–6: pick a direction

By month 6, you should have:

  • A finished Proto 1 with a draft paper.
  • A working corrective head MVP with results.
  • Smoke-test results on ACE2 and on ensemble construction.
  • A clear sense of which of these felt most rewarding to work on.

Make the thesis-direction decision after this point, not before. The data from the six-month period will tell you which option fits your taste much more reliably than abstract reasoning ever could.

10.5 The bonus item — talk to people

Independently of the technical work above, spend a few hours each month doing the following:

  • Read one paper each from Lam et al. (GraphCast), Pathak et al. (FourCastNet), Bouallègue et al. (probabilistic evaluation of AI weather), Vannitsem (statistical post-processing), and Watt-Meyer (ACE2). These are the canonical references.
  • Email two or three authors of papers you found interesting and ask short, specific questions. This builds connections that matter later.
  • Find one or two PhD students at other institutions working on adjacent things. Discord/Slack channels exist (AI for Science, Climate Change AI). The peer network is enormously useful and most students underinvest in it.

11. Closing: how to actually decide

If I had to give the single most useful piece of advice in this document, it would be this:

The thesis-shape decision is less important than getting good at the day-to-day work that the option requires. Pick the option whose day-to-day work — the actual hours-spent-doing-things — most matches the kind of researcher you want to become. Then trust that the research will sort itself out as long as you keep producing real artifacts.

Concretely:

  • If you find yourself energized by running careful comparisons and producing diagnostic figures, you're an Option A person.
  • If you find yourself energized by training new models and making things work better, you're an Option B person.
  • If you find yourself energized by the meta-question of how methods should be chosen, you're an Option C person.
  • If you find yourself energized by reading theory papers and asking why things work the way they do, you're an Option D person.

The next six months will tell you which of these descriptions actually fits you. Don't decide before then. And don't decide based on what would impress a hypothetical committee — decide based on what you find yourself wanting to do at 11pm on a Sunday.

The PhD is long. The only thing that gets you through it sustainably is doing work you actually want to do.
