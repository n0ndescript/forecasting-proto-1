Proto 1 → PhD: an honest take

Companion note to WRITEUP.md and PROCESS.md · 2026-05-28

Proto 1 alone is not a PhD. And the perplexity you're feeling is correct, not a problem.

What Proto 1 actually is: roughly three days of focused engineering that validates a precondition for a research program. It demonstrates you can stand up a real ML pipeline, make and defend technical choices, recover from infrastructure obstacles, and produce clear scientific findings. That is qualifying-exam-shaped work. It is the seedcorn for a thesis, not the thesis itself.

Three reasons not to worry about the gap

  1. It should feel like the start of a road, not the end of one. If Proto 1 felt like enough for a PhD, you would either be vastly overestimating what you built today or vastly underestimating what a PhD needs to be. The reaction "this is interesting but I don't see how it is three more years of work" is exactly the reaction a healthy first-year would have at this stage.
  2. You replicated a known pattern in a new domain. AI weather models are known in the literature to have narrow predictive distributions (Bouallègue 2024; Lam 2023 on GraphCast; Bonev 2023 on FourCastNet). You have shown this specifically for AIFS over the Indian monsoon. That is incremental contribution — worth a workshop paper or a section of a journal article. It is not, by itself, a thesis.
  3. The thesis is what comes next, fanning out from this point. Proto 1 is the seed. The thesis is the program of work that grows from it.

What that program of work plausibly looks like

A few candidate shapes; pick one or hybridize. None are right or wrong — each is a defensible doctoral path, and which one is best depends on which question grabs you and which adviser you have.

Option A — AI weather model evaluation for tropical convective systems

~4 chapters; the most direct extension.

  • Ch 1. Bias evaluation framework — multi-model (AIFS + GraphCast + Pangu + maybe FourCastNet-Spherical), multi-year (5+ years), multi-region (Indian + West African + Maritime Continent monsoons).
  • Ch 2. Causal decomposition — what drives the bias? Loss-function ablation, training-data smoothing, architectural comparison.
  • Ch 3. Corrective head as a downstream remedy.
  • Ch 4. Transfer / cross-region generalization of corrective heads.

Evaluation-heavy. Some committees love it, some say it is not architecturally novel enough. ~3 years.

Option B — Corrective heads as a methodological contribution

~4–5 chapters; the most product-shaped path.

  • Ch 1. Motivation — Proto 1 condensed.
  • Ch 2. Tabular MVP plus ablations (loss functions, feature sets, region holdouts).
  • Ch 3. Novel architecture — spatial-temporal context, possibly diffusion-based generative correction that handles the tail miscalibration properly.
  • Ch 4. Cross-model, cross-region transfer.
  • Ch 5. Operational deployment / cost-benefit / what this means for end users.

Methodologically novel if Ch 3 actually produces something new. Risk: if the v1 MVP works "well enough," there is less room for chapters 3–5 to differ meaningfully.

Option C — The Hunt-shaped "diagnostic studies" thesis

Proto 1 + Proto 2 + Proto 3 + a synthesis chapter that compares them + a "what to build" chapter. A doctoral committee might respond "interesting survey, but where is your contribution?" or might respond positively to the breadth. Depends on the committee.

Option D — A more architectural / theoretical pivot

Start from Proto 1's finding that AIFS produces a conditional-mean predictor (narrow distribution). The interesting PhD question buried here is why? Is it the MSE training objective? The lack of stochasticity in the architecture? The smoothed training data? Each is a real research question. A thesis along these lines reframes the work as fundamental methodology rather than applied evaluation.

What to actually do at the moment

The pragmatic move right now is probably not to commit to one of these. Pick one or two candidate chapters from any of the above (most obvious: corrective head MVP + multi-model evaluation), do the work, see what's interesting. The thesis shape will reveal itself in 6–12 months as you figure out which question grabs you most and where the most non-obvious results live.

One more thing worth saying

Hunt almost certainly knows Proto 1 alone is not a PhD. He is framing it as "do this, see what you find, then we figure out what's worth scaling up." That is a healthy mentorship pattern — short prototypes to test directions before committing to multi-year programs. Your job at this stage isn't to make Proto 1 into a thesis; it is to figure out which follow-on most plausibly could become a thesis chapter, and produce that thing next.

If I had to bet on which follow-on most cleanly extends Proto 1 into research-shaped work, it is the corrective head with multi-model and multi-region transfer evaluation — because it combines a tangible deliverable, a novel-enough methodological contribution (transfer learning for weather model correction is not well-explored), and direct exploitation of the structure you just found. That is likely two chapters of an eventual thesis.

Happy to keep talking about any of this — including pushing back if you think I'm being too pessimistic or too prescriptive about thesis shape.
